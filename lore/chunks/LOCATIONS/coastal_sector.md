## Overview

The **Coastal Sector** (designated **Sector C** in Terminal 7-B records) encompasses the entire shoreline perimeter of Degenora Island, stretching approximately 47 kilometers from the jagged Northern Cliffs to the sheltered coves of the Eastern Shore. This zone represents the intersection of terrestrial and marine ecosystemsâ€”where specimens adapted to land meet those transformed for aquatic existence.

Unlike the enclosed Wetlands or the contaminated Main Facility, the Coastal Sector is defined by its exposure. To the east lies the open ocean; to the west, the island interior. This liminal space has produced some of the most dramatic behavioral adaptations observed in Degenora's specimen populations.

## Geography

### Northern Cliffs (C-01 to C-12)

The island's most dramatic elevation change, where volcanic rock meets the sea in sheer drops ranging from 20 to 80 meters. The cliffs are riddled with caves and submerged tunnelsâ€”access points for specimens that have abandoned terrestrial existence entirely.

Notable features:
- **The Breathing Caves**: A network of coastal caverns where air pressure changes create rhythmic whooshing sounds. Deep Dwellers use these as communal resting sites during low tide.
- **Watchpoint North**: Observation House 02 was established here in Year 1 but abandoned in Year 3 after repeated "vocal challenges" from cliff-dwelling specimens.

### Eastern Shore (C-13 to C-28)

A gentler coastline characterized by shingle beaches and tidal mudflats. This is where most coastal specimens transition between water and landâ€”where the Deep Dwellers drag themselves ashore and the Tide Walkers patrol their territories.

Notable features:
- **The Strandline**: The debris-rich boundary between high and low tide. Specimens forage here for biological material, and Frugowski has recovered fragments of pre-Incident equipment washed up from underwater ruins.
- **Tide Pools**: Micro-ecosystems trapped in volcanic rock depressions. Several house miniature specimen populations that have adapted to these isolated conditionsâ€”evolutionary experiments in real-time.

### Southern Inlets (C-29 to C-38)

Sheltered coves where the island's interior drainage meets the sea. Brackish water conditions have created unique hybrid zones where freshwater and saltwater-adapted specimens interactâ€”sometimes peacefully, sometimes not.

Notable features:
- **The Mixing Pools**: Areas where underground freshwater springs create zones of reduced salinity. Several specimen types prefer these conditions, making the Southern Inlets unexpectedly biodiverse.
- **Kelp Forests**: Submerged groves of mutated kelp that extend up to 200 meters offshore. Deep Dwellers hunt within these forests, using the kelp as cover and ambush points.

### The Shallows (C-39 to C-47)

The western coastal zone, characterized by gradual seabed slopes and sandbar formations. This is where most human-originated specimens first enter the waterâ€”where the transformation from terrestrial to aquatic existence begins.

Notable features:
- **The Sandbar Maze**: A shifting network of underwater ridges that creates protected channels during low tide. Specimens use these as travel corridors, and Frugowski has observed coordinated group movements through these passages.
- **Beaching Sites**: Locations where Deep Dwellers intentionally strand themselves during storm eventsâ€”apparently to experience atmospheric conditions, though their exact motivation remains unknown.
