## The Pond Before

Green Pond was unremarkable once. A shallow depression in the wetlands, fed by underground springs, home to common amphibiansâ€”frogs, toads, the usual swamp-dwellers. The researchers from the original expedition noted them in their preliminary surveys: *"Rana clamitans, standard green frogs. Population approximately 200. No anomalous characteristics observed."*

They were wrong about the population count. But they were right about one thing: there was nothing anomalous. **Yet.**

## The Signal Arrives

Year 0. The Incident. The day everything changed.

The Signal didn't arrive gradually. It didn't build up or give warning. One moment, the island was silent in the way islands are silentâ€”birds, wind, water. The next moment, the air itself was **vibrating** with something that wasn't sound. Something that bypassed ears and went straight to the nervous system.

Dr. Frugowski, the only survivor of the original research team who would speak about those first hours, described it in his private journals (recovered from his quarters after his disappearance):

> *"It wasn't a sound. It was a... presence. Like standing next to a bell the size of a mountain, feeling it ring without hearing it. The ground didn't shake. The air didn't move. But something was broadcasting. Something vast and ancient and **curious**."*

The frogs in Green Pond were the first to respond.

## The Transformation

It happened over three days. The researchers almost missed itâ€”they were distracted by their own equipment failures, their own strange symptoms, the growing realization that they couldn't leave. The island wouldn't let them.

But the frogs were changing.

**Day 1:** The green frogs stopped croaking. All of them. At once. The wetlands went silent in a way that was immediately, viscerally wrong. Researchers reported headaches, nosebleeds, a persistent ringing that wasn't quite tinnitus.

**Day 2:** The frogs began to gather. Not in the random distributions typical of their species, but in deliberate patterns. Concentric circles at the center of Green Pond. Hundreds of them, sitting motionless, facing inward. Toward what? Nothing visible. But something they could feel.

**Day 3:** The chorus.

## The Signal's Purpose

Why? Why transform common frogs into... this?

The prevailing theory, developed by Kermit himself (though he refuses to explain how he knows), is that the Bog Hoppers are **receivers**. Antennae. The Signal uses them to extend its range, to process information at a granular level.

Each Bog Hopper carries a fragment of the Signal's awareness. They are not intelligent in the way humans understand intelligence. But they are **attuned**. Sensitive to fluctuations in the Signal's broadcast. When the Signal grows stronger, the Bog Hoppers become more active, more vocal, more... present.

When the Signal weakens, they go dormant.

> *"They're like the island's nervous system,*" Kermit explained in a rare moment of clarity. "*Every Bog Hopper is a nerve ending. They feel what the island feels. They know when something's wrong, when something's changed. And they sing about it. Constantly. You just have to learn to listen."*
